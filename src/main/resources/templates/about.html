RadCheck FAQ
What is the purpose of RadCheck?
RadCheck is designed to keep users safe from the harmful effects of prolonged exposure to ionizing background radiation. This data is intended to be free and accessible to anyone. Immediate results on a single location can be found with a map search or latitude/longitude coordinates, and daily updates of saved locations are made available by signing in with an account.
Where does RadCheck's data come from?
Radiation data comes from the Safecast database, which is made up of volunteer efforts to measure radioactivity all around the world. Air quality index and weather information comes from AirVisual. Map searches are done via Google Maps.
How are radiation doses measured?
Human doses of ionizing radiation are measured with the microsievert per hour unit, or µSv/h. It is the international standard for measuring the health effects of low levels of background radiation. There are sources of ionizing radiation everywhere, from the food you eat to cosmic rays from outer space. This unit does not discern between different types of ionizing radiation like alpha, beta and gamma particles.

The Environmental Protection Agency recommends to the general public to not exceed 1000 µSv per year, which translates into about 2.74 µSv per day or 0.1141 µSv/h. This is the threshold of what RadCheck considers to be a safe dose. However, your definition of safe might be completely different. It is up to you to make informed choices with the data presented by each result. Read more about Sievert units here.
How do I look up a location?
Locations can be looked up either with a map search , or by manually entering latitude and longitude coordinates. When viewing a result while logged in, you can save it to your account. Afterwards, you can see this location's result again by clicking on the "View" button next to it.
How are locations and measurements saved?
Every location has data about the coordinates and its environmental conditions. A search will try to find measurements within a one kilometer radius from the coordinates, which encompasses about 1.2 square miles. RadCheck will attempt to find the latest measurements available within the given area. When a location is saved, it is assigned a 500 meter by 500 meter bounding box, which is an area roughly the size of 3 city blocks. When a user attempts to save a location within this bounding box, they are instead directed to save the location already in the database. Users will also be prevented from saving the same location twice.

Results from a location are saved within the database for twenty-one hours, after which they are eligible to be refreshed again. Every location you save to your account will say whether or not the results are refreshed. Since saved locations are shared among all users, it only takes one search for the refreshed results to be made available to everyone.
Why are you telling me how many bananas I would have eaten in three hours?
The potassium in bananas contain trace amounts of naturally occurring ionizing radiation. Eating one banana is an equivalent radiation dose of one-tenth of one microsievert, known as a BED. This is not meant to be used as a formal measurement of radioactivity, but rather as a quick shorthand for how much low-level radiation is in an area.
Does RadCheck cost money?
Nope, it's free! RadCheck will never require payment or sell your information. However, hosting RadCheck does cost money, so if you would like to help pay for server costs, consider donating.

How does RadCheck use my information?
RadCheck only requires your email address in order to make an account. Cookies used on this site include a Remember Me cookie for easy login access (which can be opted out of at any time), and Google Analytics cookies to track user behavior. As of now, there are no mailing lists associated with RadCheck, and your email address will not be sold to third parties. Please refer to the privacy policy for details.
